# Canada’s 2018-2020 National Action Plan On Open Government

## Document

- Title: `Canada’s 2018-2020 National Action Plan on Open Government`
- Publisher: Treasury Board of Canada Secretariat
- Publication date: December 17, 2018
- Source PDF: `https://publications.gc.ca/collections/collection_2019/sct-tbs/BT22-130-2018-eng.pdf`

## Why This Document Matters

This is the government’s formal response document for the 2017-2018 consultation cycle.

If the "What We Heard" report is the government’s synthesis of the input, this action plan is the government’s statement of what it says it will actually do.

For Broadly benchmarking, this document is useful because it shows:

- which consultation themes were converted into commitments
- what kinds of action the government considered realistic or politically viable
- how the government reframed broad public concerns into programmatic milestones and lead departments

## Government Framing Of The Plan

The plan presents open government as:

- transparency
- accountability
- citizen participation

The government says the plan:

- builds on the first three national action plans
- launches `10` new commitments
- is the product of its most ambitious public engagement process to date
- reflects input from more than `10,000` people
- supports Canada’s role as co-chair of the Open Government Partnership

It also frames the plan around three international priorities:

- inclusion
- participation
- impact

This matters because the plan is not just a domestic response document. It is also written as an international leadership artifact.

## The Government’s Meta-Claim

The plan’s strongest overall claim is that it turns public comments into commitments.

It says the final commitments were developed from:

- the public engagement process
- the Multi-Stakeholder Forum on Open Government
- feminist and inclusive peer review
- collaboration with departments across government

It also says draft commitments were made public and then revised after nearly `600` further comments and ideas.

So the plan is explicitly claiming a co-creation pipeline, not a closed-door policy response.

## Important Process Context The Plan Admits

One of the more useful parts of the document is that it does not present the process as fully solved.

The Multi-Stakeholder Forum letter inside the plan says:

- the forum had become central to developing the plan
- the first year of the process was a learning year
- there were real challenges to meaningful collaboration and co-creation
- the government needed stronger commitment to the forum process across departments
- open government should not be treated as a box-checking exercise
- from the civil-society side, there was frustration that the process was not fully ready to incorporate contributions as deeply as hoped
- the forum did not reach consensus on the plan’s direction or on how it should be developed

This is important benchmark material because the plan itself records friction between consultation ideals and institutional reality.

## Overall Shape Of The Government Response

The final plan is organized into ten commitment areas:

1. user-friendly open government
2. financial transparency and accountability
3. corporate transparency
4. digital government and services
5. open science
6. healthy democracy
7. access to information
8. feminist and inclusive dialogue
9. reconciliation and open government
10. open government community

This is how the government chose to convert the consultation into an action architecture.

## Reading The Action Plan As A Response To Consultation Input

The action plan is not a list of all demands raised in consultation. It is a narrower selection of action buckets.

In effect, the government appears to do the following:

- identify large recurring concern areas
- merge adjacent concerns into a smaller number of strategic commitments
- define milestones that are administratively achievable inside existing institutions
- assign lead departments and rough implementation timelines

That means Broadly comparisons should not expect a one-to-one mapping from public themes to commitments. The relevant comparison is whether the same major concerns survive the conversion into official action language.

## Commitment 1: User-Friendly Open Government

This commitment area responds to the broad theme that openness is not useful if people cannot find, understand, or work with what government publishes.

The plan says government will:

- improve `open.canada.ca`
- help Canadians learn about open government through materials and sessions
- improve open data quality
- expand the `Open by Default` pilot for working documents, subject to privacy, confidentiality, and security limits
- co-create a public digital collaboration space for citizens and public servants
- develop privacy-protective open data guidelines
- help Canadians understand the data and models used to design and study programs

What this says about the government’s response:

- the consultation was not interpreted only as a demand for more publication
- it was interpreted as a demand for better usability, education, discoverability, and contextualization

## Commitment 2: Financial Transparency And Accountability

This commitment area is one of the clearest answers to consultation input on spending transparency and procurement.

The plan says government will:

- make budget and spending information easier to find and understand
- publish GBA+ for new announced budget expenditure and tax measures in future budgets
- ensure Canadians have access to open procurement data
- explore adoption of common contracting data standards across Canada

This appears to be the government’s direct response to demands for:

- clearer budget-cycle transparency
- better spending tools
- more accessible procurement information
- alignment with open contracting standards

## Commitment 3: Corporate Transparency

This commitment area centers on beneficial ownership transparency.

The plan says government will:

- implement legislative amendments requiring federal corporations to hold accurate and up-to-date beneficial ownership information and eliminate bearer shares
- work with provinces, territories, and stakeholders on options for improving timely access to beneficial ownership information, including possible disclosure obligations and a public registry option
- support coordinated implementation of the agreement to strengthen beneficial ownership transparency

This is a more specific anti-corruption and anti-opacity response than a generic transparency commitment.

## Commitment 4: Digital Government And Services

This commitment cluster reinforces the consultation signal that digital government and open government were converging.

The plan says government will:

- create a digital policy for the Government of Canada
- develop a data strategy roadmap for the federal public service
- engage Canadians on digital and data transformation
- create a performance dashboard for service to Canadians
- publish analytics on Canada.ca website traffic in a timely manner
- prioritize open source code in development and procurement of digital solutions
- improve transparency and awareness of AI-supported public services

This is one of the strongest examples of the government broadening "open government" into service delivery, digital governance, platform transparency, and technical design choices.

## Commitment 5: Open Science

This commitment area treats openness in the scientific domain as both publication and communication.

The plan says government will:

- develop an Open Science Roadmap
- pilot an open science portal for federal scientists’ publications
- launch a platform that helps Canadians find federal science professionals, their publications, and what they are working on
- promote open science and gather stakeholder feedback on needs
- measure progress in implementing open science and the benefits for Canadians

This commitment seems to answer consultation pressure for practical access, discoverability, and real usability of government knowledge.

## Commitment 6: Healthy Democracy

This commitment area is the clearest expression of the government’s interpretation that democratic resilience had become part of the open government agenda.

The plan says government will:

- strengthen democracy and democratic institutions in advance of and after the 2019 federal election
- use the G7 Rapid Response Mechanism to identify and respond to threats to democracy
- use existing programs to support a healthy democracy
- support a healthy and reliable news ecosystem
- champion international norms for diversity of content and transparency of information online

This is important because the consultation did not just produce requests for more data. It also produced concern about democratic health, trust, information quality, and institutional resilience.

## Commitment 7: Access To Information

This commitment area is one of the most politically informative because the "What We Heard" report makes clear that participants wanted ambitious reform, while the plan operationalizes that demand through a more bounded set of actions.

The plan says government will:

- undertake a broad review of the Access to Information regime
- improve proactive publication
- increase the number of summaries of previously released ATI requests posted to `open.canada.ca` and available through informal requests
- improve the ATIP Online Request Service
- allow electronic responses where possible
- improve online guidance on how to make ATI or personal information requests
- improve transparency about personal information banks by making them searchable on `open.canada.ca`

Interpretation:

- the government acknowledges ATI as a major theme
- the plan offers a mix of legislative review, service improvements, and publication improvements
- the response is meaningful but more administratively bounded than the strongest reform demands in the consultation summary

That tension should be preserved in any benchmark comparison.

## Commitment 8: Feminist And Inclusive Dialogue

This commitment area responds to repeated concerns that openness can reproduce exclusion unless participation and data practices are intentionally redesigned.

The plan says government will:

- test best practices for inclusive dialogue and engagement
- build public-service skills for designing and facilitating inclusive dialogue
- implement GBA+ in public engagement and consultations
- engage Canadians on gender equality through a national roundtable on GBA+
- increase access to gender and inclusion data
- create a feminist open government strategy
- fund research on gender equality and openness
- conduct GBA+ for all commitments in the plan

This is a notable move from generic openness to a more explicit inclusion lens.

It also shows that the government interpreted consultation input not only as a demand for new topics, but as a demand to change how consultation itself is designed.

## Commitment 9: Reconciliation And Open Government

This commitment area addresses the consultation signal that Indigenous data governance and the relationship between openness and Indigenous rights require distinct treatment.

The plan says government will:

- work with Indigenous peoples to advance open government
- build capacity for Indigenous communities and organizations to use data and research for their own requirements and needs
- work with Indigenous peoples to identify how transparency around consultation and engagement activities can be enhanced

This is one of the clearest places where the government acknowledges that open government cannot simply be applied as a generic one-size-fits-all transparency model.

## Commitment 10: Open Government Community

This commitment area is partly inward- and outward-facing. It speaks to community-building, international leadership, standards, and capacity.

The plan says government will:

- demonstrate leadership during Canada’s term as lead government co-chair of the OGP Steering Committee
- support the OGP Thematic Partnership on Open Data
- support international events for peer learning and impact measurement
- build capacity for more feminist open government initiatives worldwide
- expand use of the Open Data Charter
- promote data literacy and management among public servants

This is the least directly "citizen-facing" of the commitment areas, but it shows how the government understands open government as an ecosystem and field-building project, not only a domestic service promise.

## What The Government Says It Is Doing Differently

Across the plan, several recurring implementation moves appear:

- make platforms easier to use
- improve data quality and discoverability
- publish more information proactively
- create dashboards, standards, and searchable formats
- run engagement more inclusively
- build capacity inside government
- coordinate with provinces, territories, international partners, and civil society

The action plan is therefore not framed as a single reform. It is a portfolio of platform improvements, standards work, engagement practice changes, policy review, and capacity building.

## Guiding Principles The Government Elevates

The plan and its surrounding material also emphasize broader principles that cut across commitments:

- accessibility
- inclusion
- collaboration
- gender equality
- reconciliation

This matters because some of the tensions visible in the consultation process were not resolved by creating separate commitments alone. The government also chose to elevate some of them as cross-cutting principles.

## Where The Plan Is Strongest

Judging only from the document itself, the plan is strongest where it converts public concerns into visible, administratively legible steps:

- portal usability and open-data quality
- spending and procurement transparency
- beneficial ownership transparency
- digital policy, data strategy, and service dashboards
- ATI service and publication improvements
- inclusive engagement practice

These are concrete enough to track and compare.

## Where The Plan Is More Partial Or Aspirational

Other areas read as more tentative, exploratory, or ecosystem-building:

- healthy democracy
- reconciliation and open government
- community and international leadership
- some inclusion and feminist open government work

That does not make them unimportant. It means the plan often responds to large concerns with process commitments, dialogue commitments, or pilot-style actions rather than hard structural reform.

## Benchmark Tensions Worth Preserving

When comparing Broadly outputs against this plan, several tensions matter:

### Consultation Demand Versus Administrative Action

The consultation summary often records broad or ambitious concerns. The plan often translates them into:

- pilots
- reviews
- standards exploration
- dashboard creation
- training
- process improvements

That narrowing is itself analytically important.

### Actionable Commitments Versus Structural Critique

The plan is better at saying what departments will build, review, publish, or pilot than at resolving deeper structural criticisms, especially around:

- the strength of ATI reform
- the institutional depth of co-creation
- the full implications of reconciliation for data governance
- long-term democratic resilience

### Inclusion As Both Topic And Method

The plan treats inclusion in two ways:

- as its own commitment area
- as a principle that should shape all commitments

Broadly comparisons should preserve that dual role.

## Comparison Hooks For Broadly

Useful comparison questions include:

- does a Broadly report recover the same ten-ish major issue areas, even if it labels them differently?
- does it preserve the merge between digital service quality and openness?
- does it identify ATI reform as a major public concern and distinguish that from the government’s narrower response?
- does it highlight financial/procurement transparency and beneficial ownership as important response areas?
- does it recognize inclusive dialogue and reconciliation as structural design questions rather than decorative themes?
- does it notice where the government answered big concerns with pilots, reviews, or capacity-building instead of more transformative action?

## Bottom Line

This action plan is the government’s official answer to the consultation cycle.

Its central claim is:

- thousands of people participated
- their comments were synthesized into commitments
- the government is acting through ten commitment areas with named leads and milestones

In practical terms, the plan says the government is taking action on:

- usability of openness
- spending and procurement transparency
- corporate transparency
- digital government modernization
- open science
- democratic resilience
- access to information
- inclusive dialogue
- reconciliation-related openness and data collaboration
- community and international leadership

For Broadly benchmarking, this document is most useful not as ground truth, but as the government’s own official action framing: what it decided counted as a response, what it chose to prioritize, and where it translated broad public input into narrower administrative commitments.
