# Open Government Consultations 2017-2018

## Document

- Title: `Summary report on open government consultations October 2017 - August 2018`
- Publisher: Treasury Board of Canada Secretariat
- Publication date: 2019
- Source PDF: `https://publications.gc.ca/collections/collection_2019/sct-tbs/BT22-219-2018-eng.pdf`

## Why This Document Matters

This is the government’s own "what we heard" summary for the consultation cycle that fed into Canada’s 2018-2020 National Action Plan on Open Government.

For Broadly benchmarking, this document is useful as the government’s official synthesis layer. It tells us:

- how the government described the consultation process
- what themes it said were most salient
- what shifts in public input it considered important
- how it linked consultation input to the final action plan

It is not a neutral raw-data document. It is an interpretive summary produced by the same institution responsible for turning consultation input into commitments.

## Government Framing Of The Report

The report says its purpose is to:

- explain the dialogue process
- summarize what Canadians said
- describe the analysis process used by government

The government explicitly positions National Action Plans as a way to communicate how it is responding to public priorities and concerns, and as a way for citizens to hold it accountable for progress.

The report also points readers to:

- the final 2018-2020 National Action Plan on Open Government
- the raw consultation datasets

That makes this document a bridge between raw consultation input and the government’s chosen commitments.

## What The Government Says About The Engagement Process

The report presents the engagement process as broad, inclusive, and multi-channel.

It describes a six-phase engagement cycle:

1. planning
2. shaping
3. drafting
4. reviewing
5. finalizing
6. implementing

The government says the process involved:

- `8,214` questionnaire participants who helped set broad priorities
- `1,384` participants across `49` online and in-person events
- `1,414` participants contributing ideas or comments online through `open.canada.ca`, Twitter, and Reddit

The report repeatedly emphasizes that the public was involved throughout the cycle, not only at the end.

## Process Design Choices The Government Highlights

The report stresses several design choices:

- using both online and in-person channels
- trying to meet communities where they already were
- participating in third-party community events
- using thematic sessions rather than only broad generic workshops
- trying to increase the diversity of participants compared with earlier cycles

The government also says it:

- collected and transcribed written, emailed, and spoken comments
- built a master dataset of input
- categorized comments by theme

That is useful benchmark context because it shows the government itself treated the corpus as something to be aggregated, themed, and interpreted.

## Claimed Scale And Coverage

The report claims participation from a broad range of sectors:

- academia
- private sector
- public sector
- charitable and not-for-profit sector
- media
- individual citizens

It also highlights specific efforts to engage:

- Indigenous peoples
- the African Nova Scotian community
- participants across both official language communities

The report lists in-person public workshops in cities including:

- Calgary
- Edmonton
- Fredericton
- Halifax
- Kitchener-Waterloo
- Montreal
- Ottawa
- Quebec City
- Regina
- Toronto
- Vancouver
- Victoria
- Whitehorse
- Winnipeg

The government is clearly trying to make the case that the engagement was geographically broad and more diverse than earlier iterations.

## Core Interpretation Of What Changed Since Earlier Cycles

One of the most useful parts of the document is not the raw theme list, but the government’s own explanation of what had shifted since prior open government consultations.

The report highlights three especially important changes.

### 1. Digital Meets Open

The government says digital government and open government were increasingly blending together in public input.

Its summary of this shift:

- people increasingly understood access, usability, web architecture, and service design as part of openness
- findability and user experience came up across many sessions
- people wanted not only raw data, but context, navigation, and a human layer around data and services

One concrete signal the report calls out is that one of the top needs in the large questionnaire was being able to find the right person to talk to.

This matters for benchmarking because a Broadly report that frames the corpus only as open-data transparency may miss the government’s own conclusion that usability and service experience were now central.

### 2. Evolving Open Data Use Cases

The report indicates that the public conversation around open data had matured beyond simply releasing datasets.

The government says people wanted:

- more contextual information around datasets
- clearer linkage to data owners
- more practical use cases
- better quality, discoverability, and usability
- stronger links between open data and real-world problem solving

This is a notable move away from a purely supply-side open data framing.

### 3. Protecting Healthy Democracy

The report identifies a stronger concern with democratic resilience than in earlier cycles.

The government’s interpretation is that participants were increasingly concerned with:

- democratic decline
- information quality
- trust
- transparency around decision-making
- the need to protect institutions and public discourse

This is important because it broadens the consultation from technical transparency questions into democratic legitimacy and resilience.

## Thematic Structure The Government Extracted

The report says it categorized comments into themes and then mapped those themes toward the eventual commitments.

A key section explains how the final action-plan commitments correspond to what the government heard across the consultation themes. The summary is not just "these were the issues." It is "these were the issues, and here is how we bundled them into commitments."

The report highlights themes including:

- open dialogue and open policymaking
- open data
- financial transparency and accountability
- open information
- healthy democracy
- feminist and inclusive open government
- reconciliation and open government
- leadership and collaboration

The document also notes that some final commitments cut across more than one input theme.

That is important for evaluation because the government is not claiming a one-theme-to-one-commitment mapping. It is already performing a merge-and-interpret operation.

## What The Government Says On Open Dialogue And Open Policymaking

This appears as one of the most heavily commented topic areas in the report.

The government says participants wanted:

- more frequent engagement
- more meaningful engagement
- broader inclusion of marginalized communities
- involvement not just at idea generation, but through implementation and evaluation
- accountability for how public input is used
- clearer communication back to communities after engagement

The report characterizes public input as wanting genuinely open dialogue rather than performative consultation.

Government response implied in the summary:

- public engagement appears in multiple commitments
- the government says it will publish consultation data
- the government says it is working on a consultation data standard
- the government says departments should publish results of engagement and explain how input influenced policies, programs, and services

This is highly relevant to Broadly because it gives an official benchmark for traceability expectations:

- publish the data
- explain how input was used
- improve the transparency of the consultation process itself

## What The Government Says On Financial Transparency

The report describes requests for:

- budget and spending information in plain language
- tools to search and explore spending by theme or region
- stronger links across the budget cycle, estimates, procurement, and public accounts
- adoption or alignment with open contracting data standards

Government response described in the report:

- improve timeliness, completeness, and transparency of budget and Main Estimates information
- expand and improve GC InfoBase
- pilot open contracting data approaches aligned with the Open Contracting Data Standard

This is a fairly concrete example of the government translating public suggestions into bounded action items.

## What The Government Says On Open Information And Access To Information

This is one of the clearest places where the report records both demand and tension.

The report says:

- reform of the Access to Information regime received very strong support
- the most-voted idea on `open.canada.ca` related to ATI reform
- many participants felt Bill C-58 did not go far enough, especially around exemptions
- participants wanted stronger administration, more proactive disclosure, and more meaningful reform

Government position as summarized in the report:

- a full review of the Access to Information Act would begin within a year of Bill C-58 receiving Royal Assent
- the government would improve the online request service
- the government would provide plainer language for requestors
- the government recognized that some stakeholders thought the commitments still fell short

This is valuable benchmark material because it captures a partial-response pattern:

- strong demand for deeper reform
- acknowledgment that current commitments do not satisfy all critics
- narrower implementation steps offered as the action path

## What The Government Says On Inclusion, Feminism, And Reconciliation

The report does not treat inclusion as an add-on. It treats inclusion as both:

- a matter of who gets to participate in consultations
- a matter of whether open data and open information benefit everyone

For inclusive and feminist dialogue, the report says participants raised concerns about:

- who gets to participate
- power dynamics in engagement
- barriers to participation
- whether open government benefits are distributed equitably

For reconciliation and open government, the report says participants raised concerns about:

- nation-to-nation approaches
- Indigenous data governance
- the relationship between government and Indigenous communities
- the need to understand Indigenous perspectives on openness and data collection before rushing into standard transparency patterns

The report explicitly notes that the open government team would receive OCAP training to better understand First Nations principles of Ownership, Control, Access, and Possession in relation to data and information work.

That is a meaningful signal that the government recognized a tension between generic openness and Indigenous data sovereignty.

## How The Government Says It Turned Input Into Commitments

The report does not present itself as a passive note-taker. It presents a pipeline:

1. collect input across channels
2. transcribe and aggregate it into a master dataset
3. categorize it by theme
4. use those themes, together with forum input and peer review, to shape draft commitments
5. publish draft commitments
6. gather another round of comments
7. revise into the final National Action Plan

This is close to the kind of provenance chain Broadly wants to preserve:

- corpus
- extracted themes
- synthesized findings
- action-oriented summary

## Where The Government Explicitly Signals Interpretation Rather Than Raw Reporting

This document is useful partly because it makes some interpretive moves visible.

Examples:

- it says the plan focused on fewer, higher-impact commitments
- it says some issues represented broad shifts in the open government conversation
- it says specific commitments responded to clusters of public input
- it says some concerns were acknowledged even where the resulting commitment stayed narrower than stakeholders wanted

This means a Broadly benchmark should not only compare cluster themes. It should compare:

- what was surfaced
- what was elevated as especially important
- what was translated into action
- what was acknowledged but only partially acted on

## Lessons Learned The Government Records

The final section is especially useful for benchmarking because it captures what the government thought the process still got wrong or could do better.

The report says:

- engagement improved compared with earlier years
- the process reached more people and a broader mix of society
- thematic sessions and webinars were useful
- more work was needed to reach schools, universities, and libraries
- future processes should focus more on deeper topic-specific discussion
- not every channel was equally effective
- the government should work more with community partners rather than running everything itself
- there was a need to create more space for full discussion
- civil society engagement required more care, respect for time, and better partnership
- inclusive engagement needed stronger design and sustained attention
- communication and promotion could be improved

The report therefore functions partly as a self-critique of the consultation machinery, not only as a summary of public input.

## What This Document Says The Government Believes It Heard

At a high level, the government’s own synthesis appears to be:

- people want openness to be easier to use, not just technically available
- people want more meaningful and accountable public participation
- people want stronger transparency around spending, procurement, and decision-making
- people want more ambitious access-to-information reform
- people want democratic resilience and information integrity to matter
- people want inclusion, gender equity, and reconciliation to be built into open government rather than treated as side issues

## What This Document Does Not Fully Resolve

Even though the report is detailed, it still leaves some benchmark questions open:

- how representative the participation actually was relative to the broader public
- how strongly each theme was distributed across different communities
- what was deprioritized when the government chose only 10 commitment areas
- how much of the final action plan was a strong response versus a partial accommodation

Those are exactly the kinds of questions Broadly-style report comparison should probe.

## Comparison Hooks For Broadly

When comparing a Broadly report to this official summary, useful questions include:

- does Broadly also surface the convergence of digital usability and openness?
- does Broadly identify access-to-information reform as a major area of demand?
- does Broadly preserve the tension between ambitious public demands and narrower government actions?
- does Broadly recognize inclusion and reconciliation as structural themes, not side notes?
- does Broadly surface the government’s own emphasis on better feedback loops and accountability after consultation?

## Bottom Line

This document is the government’s official "what we heard" layer.

Its main message is not only that Canadians wanted more transparency. The stronger message is that Canadians wanted:

- more usable openness
- more accountable engagement
- better access to information
- more transparent financial and procurement information
- inclusion and reconciliation built into the design of open government
- stronger democratic resilience

That is the benchmark narrative Broadly reports should be able to recover, challenge, refine, or improve on.
